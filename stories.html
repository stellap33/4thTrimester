Caitlin’s Story

View Source

After bringing her newborn son home in 2017, Caitlin expected the early days of motherhood to feel joyful, even if exhausting. Instead, she felt overwhelmed, anxious, and disconnected from her baby. “I really felt like I had someone else's child in my house,” she recalled. Caitlin was experiencing postpartum depression (PPD), a serious mental health condition that is more severe and long-lasting than the common “baby blues.” According to the CDC, about 1 in 8 women who have a live birth experience postpartum depression.

Caitlin initially believed her feelings were normal and struggled in silence while those around her focused on her newborn’s health. It took months before she spoke to a doctor and began seeking support. Her experience highlights how postpartum depression can be difficult to recognize and how easily symptoms may go unnoticed. Today, Caitlin shares her story to raise awareness and remind other mothers that they are not alone and that support and treatment are available.

Jessica’s Story

View Source

Jessica Hurley’s twins were born prematurely at 32 weeks after a traumatic delivery that required both a natural birth and an emergency C-section. While her newborn sons fought to survive in the neonatal intensive care unit, Jessica faced another overwhelming challenge: mounting medical bills. Despite having private insurance through her husband’s job, the family received bills from multiple providers and struggled to understand how much they owed. In total, the pregnancy, delivery, and NICU care left the family with nearly $38,000 in medical charges, close to a quarter of their annual income.

Jessica’s experience highlights how childbirth complications can create severe financial stress even for families with insurance. High deductibles, out-of-pocket maximums, and complex billing systems often leave middle-class families responsible for thousands of dollars in medical costs. The resulting medical debt can affect families’ financial stability and even discourage them from seeking medical care, showing how the postpartum period can involve not only physical and emotional challenges but also significant economic strain.

Emily’s Story

View Source

After the birth of her son, Emily Johnson experienced overwhelming anxiety, intrusive thoughts, and suicidal ideation within weeks of coming home from the hospital. Despite reaching out repeatedly for help, she was told the earliest mental health appointment available was months away, even after she screened positive for thoughts of self-harm. At just 15 days postpartum, Emily reached a breaking point. With the support of her family, she eventually sought emergency care and later entered a specialized mother–baby mental health program that helped her begin her recovery.

Emily’s story highlights a critical reality: postpartum mental health conditions can be severe, urgent, and life-threatening, yet many mothers struggle to access timely, specialized care. Improving care coordination, expanding maternal mental health services, and ensuring mothers receive support when they ask for help are essential to protecting the health and lives of families.
