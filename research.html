Maternal Mortality & Postpartum Health Summary

The Commonwealth Fund, 06/04/2024

View Source

The United States has the highest maternal mortality rate among high-income countries, with about 22 deaths per 100,000 births in 2022, and more than 80% of these deaths are considered preventable. Significant racial disparities persist, with Black women facing the highest risk of pregnancy-related death. Many maternal deaths occur after childbirth, particularly during the postpartum period, when complications such as severe bleeding, high blood pressure, infection, and heart conditions can arise.

Several structural factors contribute to these outcomes. The United States faces shortages of maternity care providers, and nearly 7 million women live in areas without obstetric services. Unlike most other high-income countries, the U.S. does not guarantee paid maternity leave or universal health coverage, and postpartum care services such as home visits are not widely available. Expanding health coverage through Medicaid and postpartum care access has been shown to improve maternal health outcomes, especially for Black and Latina mothers.

Medicaid & Coverage Gaps

KFF Health News, 05/29/2025

View Source

Medicaid plays a central role in maternal health in the United States, covering about 41% of all births nationwide and nearly half of births in rural communities. In several states, Medicaid finances more than half of all births, making it the largest payer for pregnancy-related care. The program helps ensure access to essential services such as prenatal care, labor and delivery, postpartum care, and breastfeeding support, while federal law protects pregnant patients from out-of-pocket costs for pregnancy-related care.

Medicaid also improves access to health coverage before and after pregnancy, which is important for managing chronic conditions like hypertension, diabetes, and obesity that can affect pregnancy outcomes. Women in states that expanded Medicaid are more than twice as likely to have health coverage before becoming pregnant, which is associated with better prenatal care and healthier birth outcomes. Most states now offer 12 months of postpartum Medicaid coverage, allowing new parents to maintain access to care after childbirth. However, many parents in states that have not expanded Medicaid still lose coverage after the postpartum period, creating barriers to ongoing care for conditions such as cardiovascular disease, hypertension, and postpartum depression.

Postpartum Mental Health

womensmentalhealth.org

View Source

The postpartum period is a time of significant emotional change for many women. About 85% experience some type of mood disturbance after childbirth, and 10–15% develop more serious symptoms of depression or anxiety. Postpartum mental health conditions are often described along a spectrum, including postpartum blues, postpartum depression, and postpartum psychosis. Postpartum blues are very common, affecting 50–85% of women in the first few weeks after delivery, and may include mood swings, tearfulness, anxiety, or irritability. These symptoms typically peak around the fourth or fifth day after birth and resolve within two weeks. If depressive symptoms last longer than two weeks, further evaluation is recommended.

Postpartum depression usually develops within the first two to three months after childbirth, though it can occur at any point after delivery. Symptoms may include persistent sadness, fatigue, sleep problems, loss of interest in usual activities, feelings of guilt or worthlessness, difficulty concentrating, and suicidal thoughts. Anxiety symptoms may also occur, including panic attacks and obsessive or intrusive thoughts. The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale, a 10-item screening questionnaire, is commonly used to help identify postpartum depression and determine when further evaluation is needed.

The most severe condition, postpartum psychosis, occurs in about 1–2 out of every 1,000 births and typically begins within the first few days or weeks after delivery. Early symptoms may include restlessness, irritability, and insomnia, followed by confusion, rapidly changing mood, disorganized behavior, delusional beliefs, or hallucinations. Hormonal changes after childbirth, along with factors such as prior depression, stressful life events, and lack of social support, can increase the risk of postpartum mental health conditions. Because untreated mood and anxiety disorders can affect both the mother and child, early screening and support during the postpartum period are important.

NIH

pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37420215

View Source

Complications during the postpartum period can pose serious risks to women’s health and may contribute to maternal illness and death. However, postpartum care often receives much less attention than pregnancy and childbirth. This study examined women’s knowledge of postpartum care and complications, their recovery practices after childbirth, barriers they faced when seeking care, and their educational needs. Researchers conducted eight focus group discussions with 54 postpartum women who had delivered at four health centers in the Sagnarigu District of Tamale, Ghana, and analyzed the conversations to identify common themes.

The study found that postpartum care was often viewed mainly as caring for the baby rather than addressing the mother’s physical and mental health. Women reported limited knowledge of postpartum danger signs, challenges accessing postpartum care, and experiences of poor mental health. Many participants also expressed a need for more education about postpartum recovery and warning signs of complications. The findings suggest that improving postpartum education and communication about maternal health could help women better recognize health risks and support safer recovery after childbirth.
